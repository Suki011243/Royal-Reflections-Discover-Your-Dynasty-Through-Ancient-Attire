üé≠ Attire Characteristics

Xia & Shang: Early clothing was utilitarian, made from hemp and leather, worn with ritual accessories like jade and bronze amulets. Shang ritual garments often included animal motifs and were used in sacrificial ceremonies.

Zhou: The Zhou introduced the concept of li (ritual propriety), which influenced attire. The shenyi was widely used‚Äîa robe with a crossed collar and wide sleeves. Color, length, and embellishments varied by social rank.

üëë Representative Figures

Yu the Great (Xia): Known for taming floods and being a virtuous ruler. He is associated with tribal chieftain garb and foundational ritual dress.

King Tang (Shang): Founder of the Shang Dynasty, established royal court rituals and ceremonies that used elaborate bronze-age regalia.

King Wu of Zhou: Founder of the Zhou dynasty, implemented Heaven's Mandate. He supported the use of ritual dress to reinforce order and cosmic harmony.

üßµ Materials and Symbols

Fabrics ranged from hemp and ramie in the Xia period to early silk production in Shang and Zhou. Embroidery became prominent in the Zhou dynasty. Symbols such as dragons (imperial power), birds (heavenly messengers), and geometric patterns were incorporated into clothing. Jade pendants and bronze accessories were used for ceremonial purposes.

üèõÔ∏è Cultural and Social Role

In the Zhou dynasty, clothing became a visible marker of hierarchy and function. Nobles, officials, warriors, and commoners all had distinct dress codes. Dress played a role in state rituals, ancestor worship, and seasonal festivals. Ritual codes were recorded in texts such as the "Zhou Li" (Rites of Zhou).
