The Qing Dynasty introduced the "Manchu-style" robes called "chaofu" and "longpao." These were narrower than Han styles, with horse-hoof cuffs and embroidered dragon symbols. The "queue" hairstyle and mandatory dress codes distinguished Manchu and Han people.

üëë Representative Figures

Emperor Kangxi: Promoted standardized court attire and Confucian governance ideals.

Empress Dowager Cixi: Known for elaborate robes with luxurious embroidery and symbolism reflecting imperial power.

üßµ Materials and Symbols

Qing garments used imperial silk, satin, and gold threads. The twelve symbols of sovereignty, including the sun, moon, and dragon, were embroidered into emperor's robes to signify authority.

üè© Cultural and Social Role

Clothing played a role in ethnic identity politics. Strict laws required Han Chinese to adopt Manchu dress and hairstyles. Robes denoted ranks and were worn in court rituals and ceremonies to express loyalty to the Qing regime.
